Floods and Blood

Global warming could also affect the weather patterns, leading to more droughts, flooding and extreme weather, such as hurricanes. This will injure and kill more people. Extreme weather also leads to issues with food supply and increases in malnutrition, putting many of the world’s poorest people at serious risk.

Rising Ocean, Melting Ice

Every global shore touches the same ocean, and the ocean is rising.

Global warming could melt the world's ice caps and glaciers, leading to an increase in sea levels. Some scientists estimate that over the next hundred years sea levels could rise by between 10 cm and 90 cm - making many coastal areas around the world uninhabitable.

In fact, 147 to 216 million people live on land that will be below sea level or regular flood levels by the end of the century, assuming emissions of heat-trapping gases continue on their current trend.

Unbearable Climate

Many species of plants and animals will find they have to adapt to survive the changing climate conditions.

In the Arctic, polar bears are at risk as they have to swim much further between ice flows which uses up a lot more energy.

Nature experts have noted that animal behaviour has changed with butterflies appearing earlier and many birds have different migration patterns than they once did.

Not Without Drought

Bouts of severe drought, heat and low humidity are becoming more extreme as the climate warms. As climate change makes hot and dry conditions – often termed “fire weather” – more common and severe, vegetation dries out and landscapes become more flammable, pushing up the odds of dangerous wildfires.

No Tomorrow For Coral

Warming that has already occurred is affecting the biological timing (phenology) and geographic range of plant and animal communities Relationships such as predator-prey interactions are affected by these shifts, especially when changes occur unevenly between species.

The oceans absorb about 27% of anthropogenic CO2 emissions, resulting in increased acidity. Even under conservative projections, coral reefs will be severely impacted.

+

Warm Today, Cold Tomorrow

Spring flower blooms, summer heat waves, freeze and thaw, growing seasons, and animal migrations are all examples of natural phenomena that follow seasonal patterns. Climate change has altered many of these patterns anywhere in the world—shifting the timing of events like spring snowmelt, creating longer wildfire and growing seasons while reducing the length of time that lakes stay frozen...

This can affect a wide range of ecological and societal processes. For example, warmer winters make it easier for agricultural pests to survive between growing seasons and create adverse conditions for crops that require winter chill hours to bear fruit the following season.
